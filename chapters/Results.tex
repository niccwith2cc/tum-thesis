\subsection{Runtime}

The following figures and table report the effect of vectorization on DiffPlan runtime.
Figure~\ref{fig:runtime-grouped} shows the mean runtime of each module across algorithm variants, the black bars represent the standard deviation given five total runs.
First, the performance of DiffPlan and DiffSpatialPlan is compared.
By far the largest improvement, exceeding 90\% improvement across all methods relative to the baseline, is the loss calculation which is indicative of the work done in Section \ref{section:VecWallCollision}.
With the vectorized DiffPlan along with the DiffSpatialPlan, attaining 60\% total mean runtime improvement compared to the original baseline.
The vectorized DiffPlan additionally benefits from the sampling vectorization with a 34\% faster runtime than the baseline, as outlined in Section \ref{section:VecSampling}.
While DiffSpatialPlan simply outperforms standard DiffPlan by about 27\%.
As expected, there is no impactful improvement in the controlling module.
Additionally, the relative speed up with respect to the baseline algorithm is also documented in Figure~\ref{fig:runtime-speedup}.
Figure~\ref{fig:runtime-speedup} analogously presents the same information as Figure~\ref{fig:runtime-grouped}, but presents the results in terms of relative speedup rather than absolute runtime.
Hence, vectorized DiffPlan and SpatialDiffPlan exhibit an approximate 2.5-fold total speedup relative to the baseline.

Figure~\ref{fig:runtime-grouped} and Figure~\ref{fig:runtime-speedup} also include the GPU-offloaded temporal DiffPlan variant described in Section~\ref{section:GPUOffloading}.
This implementation reduces total mean runtime by $30\%$ over the baseline.
Unfortunately, that gain is masked by the successful vectorization of the loss calculation module.
By contrast, planning, controlling, and backpropagation each regress relative to the baseline, with controlling showing the largest penalty at roughly $62\%$ slower execution.
Section~\ref{section:GPUOffloading} attributes this pattern to incomplete GPU migration, as the differentiable MPC controller still solves its sequential LQR sub-problem on the CPU.
Therefore, at each control cycle computations must cease on the GPU to transport relevant variables to the the host device. 
The transfer and synchronization overhead overwhelm the advantage gained in the vectorizations.
GPU offloading thus yields a moderate overall speedup, but remains inferior to CPU vectorization.

Table~\ref{tab:runtime-comparison} summarizes the mean $\texttt{/drive}$ topic publish frequency for the baseline temporal planner, its vectorized variant, and the spatial sampler DiffSpatialPlan.
Vectorization increases the publish frequency from $13.2$\,Hz to $34.12$\,Hz, corresponding to a $2.59\times$ speedup, while DiffSpatialPlan achieves $30.66$\,Hz with a $2.33\times$ speedup relative to the baseline.

\subsection{Scaling}

Figure~\ref{fig:planning-scaling} displays mean planning runtime as the number of sampled trajectories $n$ increases.
The measurement complements the vectorization described in Section~\ref{section:VecSampling} by evaluating how sampling cost grows with problem size.

The baseline temporal planner exhibits the steepest growth.
Planning time rises effectively doubling over an eightfold increase in sample count.
Meanwhile, both the vectorized temporal DiffPlan and DiffSpatialPlan remain faster than the baseline at every tested value of $n$.
The steady runtime behavior of the vectorized DiffPlan and DiffSpatialPlan confirm that efficient sampling not only lowers planning computation time but almost annuls its dependence on $n$.

The GPU-offloaded scaling follows a similar pattern, but with an added cost.
Planning time remains near $24$\,ms across the full range, which is higher than the baseline at $n = 64$ but as $n$ increases the baseline rises and overtakes the GPU planning time at around $n = 250$.
The high computational time is attributed to the GPU overhead, as the overhead cost is constant for all sizes of $n$ but the parallelism benefits from higher values of $n$ which results in no additional increase in compute time as $n$ grows.

\subsection{Achieving Real Time Computation}

Unfortunately, real-time computation on the RoboRacer hardware was not achieved yet.
This is primarily due to resource management on the NVIDIA Jetson Orin Nano which powers the vehicle.
Full GPU encapsulation was not able to be achieved and therefore the stack was too computationally intensive to be computed on the car as stated previously by Swadiryus \cite{swadiryus_DifferentiableMotionPlanning_2025}.
However, attaining another variant of the differentiable MPC which can be computed should be a priority.
Adabag et al \cite{adabag_DifferentiableModelPredictive_2025} achieved a differentiable MPC that can be parallelized and computed on the GPU.
The proposed MPC was written in JAX framework, which is similar to PyTorch, as it provides automatic differentiation for gradient propagation \cite{adabag_DifferentiableModelPredictive_2025,paszke_PyTorchImperativeStyle_2019}.

Hence, this thesis provides a step in the right direction but still falls short of the desired goal of full on-board computation.

\section{Local Sampling Planner}

\subsection{Simulation Runs}

The simulation results below compare the temporal motion planner DiffPlan, and spatial motion planner DiffSpatialPlan on three tracks during training.
Each track subsection examines the positional trajectory of the vehicle, the lateral deviation from the raceline, and lap-time table for the first and the last lap of the training.
Lateral deviation from the raceline is plotted for visual inspection and a scalar quantity is calculated and tabulated $\sum |n|$, where $n$ is the lateral deviation, to quantify the amount of total deviation per lap.
Additionally, the loss function terms: $\mathcal{L}_{\text{coll}}, \ \mathcal{L}_{\text{prog}}, \ \mathcal{L}_{\text{rl}}$ are monitored and plotted throughout all the training laps. 

\subsubsection{FTM Oval}

For each of the planning algorithms used, Figure~\ref{fig:ftm-oval-sim09-traj} depicts the vehicle's path along the track for two laps.
Looking at Tables \ref{tab:n-ftm} and \ref{tab:lap-ftm}, both planners improve upon their lap times and their total deviation as they completed their final training lap.
Overall, the DiffPlan outperforms the spatial planner in raceline adherence, confirmed by the vehicle's first lap trajectory where the vehicle immediately tries to go back to the raceline.
While the DiffSpatialPlan follows the curvature of the track to eventually meet up with the raceline.
However, DiffSpatialPlan seems to be the finishing laps faster, at 3.79 seconds in the last training lap compared to 7.25 seconds for DiffPlan.
This can be explained by looking at Figure \ref{fig:ftm-oval-sim09-v} which details the velocity profile of the vehicle for both laps, with the spatial planner pushing the car beyond the reference velocity.

\subsubsection{Yas Marina}

The Yas Marina circuit features a longer track with more intricate racing scenarios than FTM Oval.
Simulation outputs for laps~0 and~9 are displayed in Figures~\ref{fig:yas-marina-sim09-traj} to~\ref{fig:yas-marina-sim09-v}, with Tables~\ref{tab:n-yas} and~\ref{tab:lap-yas} reporting $\sum|n|$ and lap time.
Both planners reduce cumulative lateral deviation and lap time by the final training lap.

DiffPlan maintains tighter raceline tracking throughout training.
Its cumulative lateral deviation falls from $95.9$\,m at lap~0 to $49.6$\,m at lap~9, compared with $186.8$\,m and $84.6$\,m for DiffSpatialPlan.
Figure~\ref{fig:yas-marina-sim09-lat} supports this separation.
Both planners recover quickly from the same initial lateral offset, yet DiffPlan again converges toward the reference path more aggressively in the opening meters of lap~0.
Furthermore, the temporal planner produces pronounced lateral oscillations along the main straight, whereas the spatial planner stabilizes more quickly and already follows the circuit curvature.

Despite the higher deviation metric, DiffSpatialPlan records the faster laps at both checkpoints.
Lap time drops from $15.87$\,s at lap~0 to $14.38$\,s at lap~9, versus $25.86$\,s and $24.29$\,s for DiffPlan.
Figure~\ref{fig:yas-marina-sim09-v} attributes this gap to a markedly more aggressive velocity profile.
DiffPlan holds longitudinal speed near the offline reference.
DiffSpatialPlan instead accelerates to approximately $5.0$\,m/s on the straights, brakes into the hard corners near $s \approx 10$\,m, $30$\,m, and $60$\,m along the circuit length.
By lap~9 the spatial planner traces a smooth path that closely follows the raceline geometry, including the low-speed hairpin sections, while completing the circuit approximately $9.9$\,s faster than the temporal planner.

\subsubsection{Austria Track}

As the most demanding of the three simulated circuits, the Austria track benchmarks how well the planning algorithms manage to find an optimal operating point.
Figures~\ref{fig:austria-sim09-traj} to~\ref{fig:austria-sim09-v} compare planner behavior at the first and tenth training laps, and Tables~\ref{tab:n-aus} and~\ref{tab:lap-aus} collect the corresponding $\sum|n|$ and lap-time values.
Consistent with the preceding tracks, training again lowers both metrics for each planner.

Turning first to the temporal planner, lap~0 exposes repeated wall contacts, causing the vehicle to bounce off the barriers, reorient, and attempt to continue as shown in Figure~\ref{fig:austria-sim09-traj}.
A large crash event appears in Figure~\ref{fig:austria-sim09-lat} near $s \approx 70$\,m, where a sharp negative spike in the lateral offset marks the terminal barrier contact before going through the last corner to the finish line.
Over subsequent laps, the updated cost weights gradually steer DiffPlan away from the barriers, and by lap~9 the planner completes a full lap without crashing.

By contrast, DiffSpatialPlan exhibits greater robustness even from the first lap.
On lap~0 the vehicle manages to avoid crashing into the barriers and maintains a continuous path around the circuit.
By lap~9 the spatial planner hugs the raceline through the corners and produces the trajectory that most closely matches the grey reference path in Figure~\ref{fig:austria-sim09-traj}.
Nevertheless, cumulative lateral deviation remains higher for DiffSpatialPlan at both checkpoints ($259.5$\,m and $149.8$\,m versus $169.5$\,m and $121.8$\,m), reflecting the wider, speed-oriented cornering lines that the spatial sampler favors on this circuit.

In terms of lap time, the gap between the two planners is the largest observed across all three tracks.
DiffSpatialPlan records $19.95$\,s at lap~0 and $18.61$\,s at lap~9, compared with $42.51$\,s and $39.43$\,s for DiffPlan.
Figure~\ref{fig:austria-sim09-v} explains this separation.
DiffPlan holds speed between roughly $1.5$\,m/s and $3.5$\,m/s once a continuous lap becomes feasible, well below the offline reference that peaks near $11$\,m/s on the fastest segments.
DiffSpatialPlan instead sustains approximately $4$\,m/s to $5$\,m/s across most of the lap, decelerating to about $2.5$\,m/s only through the demanding mid-circuit section between $s \approx 45$\,m and $s \approx 65$\,m, and its lap~0 and lap~9 profiles already agree closely.
Overall, the Austria results highlight a clear robustness advantage for spatial sampling on a boundary-constrained circuit, while the temporal planner requires substantial training before it can complete a lap without contact, and even then remains substantially slower.

\subsection{Real Runs}

To assess the sim-to-real gap, both planners were deployed on the physical FTM Oval circuit using the setup depicted in Figure~\ref{fig:exp_setup_real}.
The same planner configuration, initial raceline offset, and training objective from the simulation study were applied on the track.
This subsection reports the corresponding hardware results and compares them against the simulated FTM Oval benchmarks above.

\subsubsection{FTM Oval}

On the physical circuit, Figures~\ref{fig:ftm-oval-real09-traj} to~\ref{fig:ftm-oval-real09-v} and Tables~\ref{tab:n-ftm-real} and~\ref{tab:lap-ftm-real} summarize behavior at laps~0 and~9.
As in simulation, both planners reduce cumulative lateral deviation and lap time over training, yet the real runs expose a substantially narrower performance spread between the two algorithms.

With respect to raceline adherence, DiffPlan again records the lower cumulative $\sum|n|$ values, falling from $21.1$\,m to $11.7$\,m compared with $80.8$\,m and $65.4$\,m for DiffSpatialPlan.
Figure~\ref{fig:ftm-oval-real09-lat} and \ref{fig:ftm-oval-real09-traj} clarifies this contrast.
Both planners correct the initial lateral offset near $0.95$\,m during lap~0, after which DiffPlan oscillates symmetrically about the reference while DiffSpatialPlan settles into a tighter radius to complete the lap.

In terms of lap time, DiffSpatialPlan still achieves the faster final lap at $6.80$\,s versus $7.73$\,s for DiffPlan, but the margin is far smaller than in simulation, where the corresponding values in Table~\ref{tab:lap-ftm} were $3.79$\,s and $7.25$\,s.
Figure~\ref{fig:ftm-oval-real09-v} shows both planners reaching a hardware limit of $2.3$\,m/s, whereas the offline reference exceeds $4.6$\,m/s along portions of the lap and the simulated spatial planner in Figure~\ref{fig:ftm-oval-sim09-v} routinely drives above the reference profile.

Regarding sim-to-real transfer, the temporal planner reproduces simulation lap times most closely, recording $8.92$\,s versus $8.84$\,s at lap~0 and $7.73$\,s versus $7.25$\,s at lap~9.
DiffSpatialPlan departs more strongly from its simulated performance, with the lap~9 time increasing from $3.79$\,s in simulation to $6.80$\,s on the physical track.
Overall, the real FTM Oval experiments confirm that both planners train successfully on hardware under motion-capture localization.
